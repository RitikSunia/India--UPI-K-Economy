# India UPI & K-Economy State Atlas — Executive Summary

**Reference period:** May 2026 (UPI state totals) · **Coverage:** 36 states & union territories · **Source:** Interactive Atlas dataset (NPCI/RBI UPI extract, MoHFW 2026 population projections, MGNREGA wage & demand series)

---

## The headline finding

India's UPI ecosystem is **national in scale but uneven in depth**. In a single reference month, states collectively logged roughly **13.0 billion transactions** worth about **₹18.3 lakh crore** — yet the typical resident averages only **~9.1 UPI transactions per month**. Big states dominate **volume**; smaller and more urbanised states often lead **intensity** (transactions per person). These are different questions, and conflating them misreads the map.

The atlas uses a **dual frame**: absolute market size versus per-capita adoption. A third lens — the **K-Economy composite score** — blends UPI intensity with rural wage levels and inverse MGNREGA demand to capture digital participation alongside labour-market conditions.

## Volume leaders vs intensity leaders

**Volume top 5:** Maharashtra (17.4%), Uttar Pradesh (9.8%), Karnataka (9.2%), Telangana (7.0%), Tamil Nadu (7.0%).

**Intensity top 5:** Goa (23.9), Delhi (23.7), Telangana (23.5), Chandigarh (22.6), Maharashtra (17.5).

Only **Maharashtra** and **Telangana** appear in both lists. High-volume states like **Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and Rajasthan** rank far lower on intensity — they move enormous payment flows but spread them across very large populations.

---

## The Goa anomaly (case study)

Goa ranks **#1 on UPI intensity** (23.9 txn/person) and **#3 on K-Economy** (92.3) despite contributing just **0.3%** of national transaction volume. With only **1.6 million** projected residents, a modest absolute transaction count produces an extreme per-capita rate. Tourism, remittances, urbanisation, and a small denominator all amplify the signal.

**Including Goa** barely shifts the national average (9.13 → 9.11 txn/person). **Excluding Goa** is useful when comparing large states or building policy targets — it prevents a micro-state outlier from framing the narrative. The atlas provides a toggle for this reason; both views are valid depending on the question asked.

---

## K-Economy: who leads, who lags

**Top composite performers:** Delhi, Chandigarh, Goa, Haryana, Karnataka, Telangana — combining strong UPI use with relatively higher wages and lower MGNREGA dependence.

**Bottom performers:** Bihar, Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand, Rajasthan — low UPI intensity, lower wages, and high rural employment-programme demand.

Rajasthan illustrates the **volume–intensity paradox**: 4th by transaction volume nationally, but **#29 on K-Economy** (28.3) with only **6.9 txn/person** — below the national average.

## Directional implications

1. **Measure depth, not just scale.** National success metrics should track per-capita adoption alongside gross transaction counts.
2. **Tailor inclusion strategies by state type.** High-volume/low-intensity states need last-mile and rural onboarding; high-intensity states are laboratories for merchant and transit integration.
3. **Treat outliers transparently.** Goa (and small UTs) should be reported with context, not silently dropped or allowed to dominate rankings.
4. **Link digital and labour indicators.** K-Economy suggests that states with weak wages and high MGNREGA demand also lag on UPI — digital inclusion is not independent of broader economic structure.
5. **Avoid single-month over-interpretation.** May 2026 is a cross-section; seasonal and festival effects matter.

---

## Limitations (brief)

One reference month; projected (not census) population; some UTs lack wage/MGNREGA series; K-Economy is a composite index, not a causal model; macro series (CPI, Sensex, GDP) provide national context only.
