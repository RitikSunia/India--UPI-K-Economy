# India's UPI Revolution at State Level: A Data Atlas Report on Digital Payment Intensity, Volume, and the K-Economy

**Prepared from:** India UPI & K-Economy Interactive Atlas  
**UPI reference month:** May 2026  
**Geographic scope:** 36 states and union territories of India  
**Report type:** Standard analytical study with executive summary companion

---

## Abstract

This report synthesises findings from a state-level atlas of India's Unified Payments Interface (UPI) ecosystem, augmented by a composite **K-Economy** indicator that links digital payment depth to rural wage levels and MGNREGA employment demand. Using a single-month cross-section of roughly 13 billion transactions, the analysis demonstrates that UPI adoption is simultaneously **nationwide** and **geographically uneven**. The central analytical contribution is a **dual frame** that separates **transaction volume** (market size) from **payment intensity** (transactions and value per person). A dedicated case study addresses the **Goa anomaly** — India's smallest state by population ranking first on per-capita UPI intensity while contributing less than one-third of one percent of national volume. The report concludes with directional policy implications, a full state ranking commentary, international and historical context, and an explicit limitations section.

---

## 1. Introduction: Why State-Level UPI Matters

India's UPI has become one of the world's largest real-time payment rails. Headline national figures — billions of transactions per month, trillions of rupees in value — rightly celebrate a remarkable infrastructure achievement. Yet national aggregates hide a more nuanced geography. **Maharashtra** alone accounts for roughly one in six UPI transactions; **Bihar** processes a comparable absolute volume but spreads it across a population more than seven times larger. The policy question is not only *how much* India pays digitally, but *where* digital payments have become habitual versus where they remain thin on the ground.

This report draws on the Interactive Atlas dataset, which integrates:

- **UPI state totals** from NPCI/RBI statewise ecosystem extracts (reference month: May 2026)
- **Population projections** for 2026 (Ministry of Health & Family Welfare)
- **MGNREGA wage rates** (FY 2022–23) and **person-days demanded** (FY 2023–24)
- **National macro context**: CPI (rural/urban), BSE Sensex monthly averages, and annual GDP growth (World Bank)

The atlas deliberately operates at **state and UT level**. District-level granularity is excluded to keep the narrative national in scope while still actionable for state planners.

### 1.1 What this report is — and is not

This is a **descriptive and diagnostic** study. It maps patterns, names outliers, and sketches directional implications. It does **not** claim causal identification — a single month's data cannot prove that higher wages *cause* higher UPI use, or that MGNREGA demand *reduces* digital adoption. The K-Economy score is a **composite index for comparison**, not a welfare metric or forecasting model.

## 2. Analytical Framework: Volume, Intensity, and K-Economy

### 2.1 The dual frame (recommended central lens)

Two metrics answer different questions:

| Lens | Question it answers | Key measures |
|------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Volume** | Where is the UPI *market* largest? | Total transactions (mn), total value (₹ cr), national share (%) |
| **Intensity** | Where is UPI *woven into daily life*? | Transactions per person per month; value per person (₹) |

**Volume** correlates with population, urban concentration, and commercial activity. **Intensity** normalises for population and reveals adoption depth — how many times per month, on average, does each resident generate a UPI transaction?

The atlas scatter view plots states on both axes. Median splits create four quadrants:

- **High volume + high intensity:** Maharashtra, Karnataka, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Delhi, Haryana, Andhra Pradesh — the mature digital economies.
- **High volume + low intensity:** Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Rajasthan, West Bengal, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Odisha, Assam — large markets with room to deepen adoption.
- **Low volume + high intensity:** Goa, Chandigarh, Ladakh, Sikkim, Puducherry, and other small or highly urbanised units — deep per-capita use but small absolute footprint.
- **Low volume + low intensity:** Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Tripura, and several northeastern states — dual laggards on both dimensions.

Policy conversations often default to volume rankings because big numbers are intuitive. This report argues that **intensity should receive equal billing** — especially when designing inclusion programmes aimed at habit formation rather than infrastructure load.

### 2.2 The K-Economy composite

The K-Economy score (0–100) combines three state-level indicators:

```
K-Economy = 40% × UPI per-capita rank
          + 30% × rural wage rank (MGNREGA, FY 2022–23)
          + 30% × inverse MGNREGA demand rank (FY 2023–24 person-days)
```

Higher scores indicate states where digital payment depth, wage levels, and lower reliance on employment guarantees align favourably. **CPI, Sensex, and GDP** appear in the atlas as **national macro context** — they do not enter the state score directly.

The formula encodes a hypothesis: digital participation, labour-market remuneration, and rural safety-net pressure are **jointly informative** about economic structure. A state with high MGNREGA demand and low UPI intensity may face compound development challenges; a state with high UPI and high wages may represent a more integrated "K-economy" — knowledge, connectivity, and cashless commerce.

### 3.1 Reading the national picture

At ~9.1 transactions per person per month, the average Indian resident initiates a UPI payment roughly **twice a week**. That is substantial — evidence that UPI is no longer a metro-only phenomenon — but far below the rates seen in India's most intensive states (20+ per person).

The **50.4% top-five concentration** means half of all state-attributed UPI volume flows through just five jurisdictions. This is not necessarily unhealthy — large economies should generate large flows — but it underscores that **national totals are heavily shaped by a handful of states**, and national averages can mask deep sub-national variation.

## 6. Case Study: The Goa Anomaly

### 6.1 The paradox in numbers

| Metric | Goa | National average |
|--------|-----|------------------|
| Population (2026 proj.) | 1.60 million | 1,426.9 million |
| UPI transactions | 38.2 mn | 13,021.6 mn |
| Volume share | **0.29%** | 100% |
| Intensity rank | **#1** | — |
| Txn/person/month | **23.9** | 9.1 |
| Value/person/month | **₹36,508** | ₹12,823 |
| K-Economy score | **92.3 (#3)** | — |
| MGNREGA person-days (lakh, FY23–24) | 0.43 | — |
| MGNREGA wage (₹/day) | 315 | — |

Goa is India's **smallest state by population** and among the smallest by area. It processes only 38 million UPI transactions in the reference month — a rounding error in national terms. Yet when divided by 1.6 million residents, Goa produces the **highest per-capita intensity in the country**, narrowly ahead of Delhi and Telangana.

### 6.2 Why Goa distorts — and what it reveals

Several structural factors inflate Goa's per-capita metrics:

1. **Small denominator effect.** Per-capita rates are mathematically sensitive when the population base is tiny. A modest absolute transaction count yields an extreme ratio.

2. **Tourism and hospitality.** Goa receives millions of visitors annually. Tourist UPI transactions may be attributed to Goa's ecosystem while the "per person" divisor counts only residents — inflating intensity relative to the resident population.

3. **Urbanisation and remittance flows.** High literacy, strong service-sector employment, and inbound transfers from Goans working elsewhere can elevate digital payment frequency among residents.

4. **Low MGNREGA footprint.** With only 0.43 lakh person-days demanded in FY 2023–24, Goa scores well on the inverse-MGNREGA component of K-Economy — reflecting economic structure, not digital policy success alone.

### 6.3 When to include Goa — and when to exclude it

The Interactive Atlas includes an **"Include Goa in analysis"** toggle because the anomaly is analytically material:

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|----------|----------------|
| National headline averages | Include Goa — impact on national mean is tiny (9.13 vs 9.11 txn/person) |
| Ranking large states for policy targets | Consider excluding Goa — prevents a micro-state from occupying #1 |
| Scatter plots and peer comparisons | Show both views — outlier visibility vs cleaner clustering |
| K-Economy composite | Include with annotation — Goa's #3 rank reflects genuine high wages + low MGNREGA + top UPI |

**Excluding Goa does not materially change national aggregates** (population-weighted intensity shifts by less than 0.02 txn/person). It **does** change who appears at the top of intensity rankings (Delhi becomes #1) and alters peer-group comparisons for states like Rajasthan or Karnataka.

The anomaly is not a data error — it is a **methodological teaching moment**. Small states and union territories (Chandigarh, Ladakh, Lakshadweep, Puducherry) exhibit similar, if less extreme, patterns. Any state-level digital index should report **both per-capita and absolute metrics**, and flag units where population < 5 million as potentially volatile.

## 8. Full State Ranking Commentary

The atlas covers all 36 states and UTs. Below, states are grouped by their position in the dual frame for narrative clarity. Exact ranks are available in the Interactive Atlas rankings tab.

### 8.1 Tier A — High volume, high intensity (digital leaders)

**Maharashtra, Karnataka, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, Delhi, Haryana, Andhra Pradesh, Kerala**

These states combine large absolute markets with above-median per-capita adoption. They are India's **UPI mature markets** — suitable for innovation pilots (transit payments, IoT micro-payments, cross-border UPI extensions) because both infrastructure and user habit exist at scale.

### 8.2 Tier B — High volume, low intensity (scale without depth)

**Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Rajasthan, West Bengal, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Odisha, Assam**

This tier contains roughly **half of India's population**. These states generate significant transaction volume but lag on per-person adoption. **Policy priority:** merchant onboarding in tier-2/3 cities, rural agent networks, local-language interfaces, and feature-phone UPI (UPI 123Pay) deployment. Volume growth here would move national totals more than any amount of optimisation in already-intensive states.

### 8.3 Tier C — Low volume, high intensity (small but deep)

**Goa, Chandigarh, Ladakh, Sikkim, Puducherry, Andaman & Nicobar, Arunachal Pradesh, Mizoram**

Per-capita leaders, often with populations under 5 million. Valuable as **case studies** but risky as **policy templates** for large states. Goa is the archetype (see Section 6).

### 8.4 Tier D — Low volume, low intensity (dual challenge)

**Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Tripura, Manipur, Meghalaya, Nagaland, Himachal Pradesh, Jammu & Kashmir**

These states face compound hurdles: smaller commercial bases and below-average adoption depth. Interventions may need to address **connectivity, literacy, and merchant acceptance** before per-capita metrics can rise.

## 9. Macro Context: India Beyond the States

### 9.1 Historical trajectory

UPI's rise is a recent phenomenon. National macro data in the atlas spans 2020–2025:

- **BSE Sensex** monthly average rose from roughly **31,000** (April 2020) to **82,500** (July 2025) — a proxy for financial-market expansion concurrent with digital payment growth.
- **GDP growth** recovered from **−5.8%** (2020 pandemic contraction) to **+6.5%** (2024), with strong rebound years in 2021 (+9.7%) and 2023 (+9.2%).

UPI adoption accelerated through the pandemic period when contactless payments became essential. The May 2026 cross-section captures a mature but still evolving system — not the early-adoption phase.

### 9.2 Rural–urban price dynamics

Latest available CPI data (July 2025) shows:

- Rural CPI: **197.6**
- Urban CPI: **194.2**
- Rural–urban gap: **+3.4 points** (rural higher)

Persistent rural inflation pressure — without corresponding wage growth in lagging states — may constrain discretionary digital spending and merchant investment in payment infrastructure. The K-Economy's inclusion of MGNREGA wages and demand is an attempt to proxy this structural divide, however imperfectly.

### 9.3 International comparison (conceptual)

India's UPI is unusual globally:

- **Account-to-account real-time retail payments** at billion-transaction-per-month scale are unmatched by any single national rail.
- China's Alipay/WeChat Pay achieved comparable scale but through **closed-loop wallet ecosystems**; UPI is **interoperable and bank-led**.
- The EU's SEPA Instant and Brazil's Pix offer parallels, but neither matches UPI's merchant ubiquity at the street-vendor level.

India's challenge is no longer **whether** UPI works — it demonstrably does — but **whether adoption depth can be equalised** across states with vastly different population sizes, urbanisation rates, and labour-market conditions.

## 10. Directional Policy Implications

This section offers **principles, not prescriptions** — consistent with the report's descriptive mandate.

### 10.1 Measure what matters

National dashboards should report **both** gross transaction volume and per-capita intensity. A state that doubles volume by adding users who transact once a month is progress; a state that doubles intensity by making existing users transact daily is a different kind of progress. Conflating the two obscures strategy.

### 10.2 Segment by state archetype

The four-quadrant framework (Section 2.1) implies differentiated approaches:

- **Tier A:** Innovate and export best practices.
- **Tier B:** Invest in last-mile inclusion — the highest national leverage.
- **Tier C:** Study but do not generalise; control for tourism and small-N effects.
- **Tier D:** Combine digital literacy, connectivity, and merchant incentives.

### 10.3 Handle outliers transparently

Goa, Chandigarh, and other micro-jurisdictions should appear in datasets with **annotations**, not deletions. The atlas toggle model — default include, option to exclude for comparative analysis — is a reasonable standard for official reporting.

### 10.4 Link digital and labour policy

States with low K-Economy scores tend to combine weak UPI intensity, low rural wages, and high MGNREGA demand. Digital inclusion programmes that ignore wage stagnation and employment insecurity may plateau. Conversely, rising UPI adoption in states with improving wage indicators (e.g., Haryana, Karnataka) suggests that **digital depth and labour-market health can co-evolve**.

### 10.5 Resist single-month narratives

May 2026 may include seasonal effects (pre-monsoon, wedding season, festival calendars). Trend analysis requires time series at state level — currently unavailable in this dataset. Policy commitments should be based on **multi-month or multi-year trajectories**, not one snapshot.

## 11. Data Sources and Methodology

| Component | Source | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| UPI transactions & value | NPCI/RBI statewise UPI ecosystem extract | Reference month: 2026-05 |
| Population | MoHFW state projections, 2026 | Not census enumeration |
| Rural wages | MGNREGA wage rates, FY 2022–23 | State-level |
| MGNREGA demand | Person-days by state, FY 2023–24 | Inverse rank in K-Economy |
| CPI | Monthly rural/urban/general indices | National |
| Sensex | Monthly BSE average | National |
| GDP growth | World Bank annual series, 2015–2024 | National |

### Key formulas

- **Transactions per person** = UPI transactions ÷ projected population
- **Value per person** = UPI value (₹ cr) × 10,000,000 ÷ projected population
- **National share** = state transactions ÷ sum of all state transactions
- **K-Economy score** = 40% UPI per-capita rank + 30% wage rank + 30% inverse MGNREGA rank (normalised to 0–100)

When Goa is excluded, national aggregates and ranks are **recomputed** from the remaining 35 states — intensity ranks, K-Economy ranks, and population-weighted averages all shift accordingly.

## 12. Limitations

A dedicated limitations section is warranted because the data are rich enough to invite over-interpretation.

1. **Single-month cross-section.** May 2026 is one snapshot. Seasonal, festival, and agricultural-cycle effects are not controlled for. No trend claims are valid without longitudinal data.

2. **Projected population.** Per-capita metrics use 2026 projections, not census counts. Migration, urbanisation, and tourist flows (especially relevant for Goa) introduce denominator uncertainty.

3. **Missing series for some UTs.** Wage and MGNREGA data are absent for several union territories, limiting K-Economy comparability.

4. **Composite index limitations.** K-Economy ranks are ordinal transformations — a state ranked 10th on each component does not necessarily score 50. The 40/30/30 weights are judgmental, not econometrically derived.

5. **No causal claims.** Correlation between UPI intensity and wages does not imply that raising wages will raise UPI adoption, or vice versa.

6. **Attribution ambiguity.** UPI transactions are attributed by state in the NPCI/RBI extract; cross-border, inter-state commuter, and tourist transactions may not align with resident population.

7. **Macro context is national only.** CPI, Sensex, and GDP cannot be disaggregated to explain state-level variation in this dataset.

8. **District blind spot.** State averages hide within-state inequality — a high-intensity state may contain low-intensity districts and vice versa.

## 13. Conclusion

India's UPI story at state level is one of **achieved scale and unfinished depth**. The national system processes more than 13 billion transactions in a single month — a figure that would have been unimaginable a decade ago. Yet the average Indian generates only nine UPI transactions per month, and the gap between the most intensive state (Goa: 23.9) and the least (Bihar: 3.9) remains wider than policymakers should accept.

The **dual frame** — volume versus intensity — is the clearest lens for reading this map. It explains why Uttar Pradesh and Rajasthan matter enormously for aggregate flows while simultaneously ranking among the weakest on per-capita adoption. It explains why Goa tops intensity rankings while barely registering on volume — and why that anomaly demands explicit treatment rather than silent exclusion.

The **K-Economy composite** adds a labour-market dimension: digital leaders tend to have higher wages and lower MGNREGA dependence; digital laggards cluster in the Hindi-belt and eastern regions with the opposite profile. This does not simplify policy — it complicates it productively, reminding us that cashless adoption is embedded in broader economic structure.

For a mixed audience of policymakers, researchers, business stakeholders, and informed citizens, the actionable takeaway is straightforward: **India has built the world's most impressive real-time payment highway. The next chapter is about who gets to travel on it every day — not just who generates the most traffic.**
